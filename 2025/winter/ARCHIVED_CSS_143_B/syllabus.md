###  COURSE DESCRIPTION

Transition from basic programming skills to a rigorous process of software development. Familiarization with higher level programming techniques (recursion, generic programming, stacks, queues, trees, searching, and sorting). Emphasizes connection between algorithmic thought and implementation. Prerequisite: minimum grade of 2.7 in CSS 161, CSS 142, or CSE 142. Co-requisite: CSSSKL 143.

###  GRADING

Your course grade will be computed as in the following:

  * In-Class Activities: 10%
  * Homework: 25%
  * Exams (15%, 25%, 25% each respectively ):  65%



Your lowest 2 in-class activities grades will automatically be dropped. You can miss 2 classes without penalty, but I strongly recommend attending all classes as there is a high correlation between not attending class and failing the course.

Your lowest 2 homework grades will automatically be dropped. You can miss 2 homeworks without penalty, but I strongly recommend completing all homeworks as they will prepare you for the exams.

In general students who engage with the course get 90-100% in activities and in homeworks. The exam grades tend to be much lower, usually around 70-80%.

A scale of 90s (3.5‐4.0), 80s (2.5‐3.4), 70s (1.5‐2.4), 60s (0.5‐1.4) is a rough guide. A student who achieves 75% will receive a minimum of 2.0, 85% will receive a minimum of 3.0 and 95% and above will receive a 4.0 grade. Scores in between will be interpolated.

If you disagree with your grade for each assignment, please bring the issue to my attention within a week after I returned the graded work to you.

**Late assignments will not be accepted**. Zybooks assignments often have multiple sections. Make sure you complete all the assignment parts. I will make allowances for exceptional circumstances such as sickness, bereavement and official university business. I will not make exceptions for work, other classes, travel, personal obligations, etc.

Other than when working in pairs, talking about code is OK, looking at each others code is not OK. Looking at references to understand how a functions gets used is OK; looking up assignment solutions is not OK. Asking ChatGPT or other AI systems to solve your assignment is not OK. It is also not acceptable for your code or solutions to be publicly accessible on the web (for example, a public GitHub repository). Plagiarism will result in an assignment score of zero and a misconduct letter in your student record. Please be very careful to adhere to the student code of conduct: <http://www.washington.edu/cssc/for-students/student-code-of-conduct/>
